How to Find Derivatives & Understand Rate of Change

Introduction to Derivatives

The derivative of a function represents the instantaneous rate of change of the function with respect to its variable. Intuitively, it describes the slope of the tangent line to the function's graph at any given point.

Common Rules for Finding Derivatives

1. Power Rule

If f(x) = xn, then:

f'(x) = n × xn - 1

2. Product Rule

If f(x) = u(x) × v(x), then:

f'(x) = u'(x) × v(x) + u(x) × v'(x)

3. Quotient Rule

If f(x) = u(x) ÷ v(x), then:

f'(x) = [u'(x) × v(x) − u(x) × v'(x)] ÷ [v(x)]2

4. Chain Rule

If f(x) = h(g(x)), i.e., a composition of functions, then:

f'(x) = h'(g(x)) × g'(x)

Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change of a function f(x) over the interval [a, b] is given by the formula:

Average Rate of Change = (f(b) − f(a)) / (b − a)

This represents the slope of the secant line connecting the points (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) on the graph.

Tangent Line Equation (Point-Slope Form)

The equation of the tangent line to the graph of f(x) at the point (x₁, y₁) with slope m (the derivative at x₁) is:

y = y₁ + m × (x − x₁)

Where:

  • y is the dependent variable (value on the tangent line)
  • x is the independent variable
  • y₁ = f(x₁) is the function value at x₁
  • m = f'(x₁) is the derivative (slope) at x₁
